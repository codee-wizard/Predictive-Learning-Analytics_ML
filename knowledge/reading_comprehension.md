# Reading Comprehension

## Main Idea

The main idea is the central point of a passage or paragraph. It is broader than
a detail but more specific than a general topic. A good main idea statement
answers: What is the author mostly trying to say?

## Supporting Details

Supporting details explain, prove, or develop the main idea. They may be facts,
examples, statistics, descriptions, or quotations. Strong readers connect each
detail back to the author's central point.

## Inference

An inference is a logical conclusion based on evidence from the text and prior
knowledge. Inference questions often ask what is implied rather than directly
stated. A valid inference must be supported by textual clues.

## Tone

Tone is the author's attitude toward the subject. Common tones include serious,
critical, humorous, optimistic, concerned, doubtful, admiring, and objective.
Students should identify tone by looking at word choice and emphasis.

## Theme

Theme is the deeper message or insight in a literary text. It is not usually a
single word. For example, "friendship" is a topic, while "true friendship
requires honesty" is a theme.

## Vocabulary In Context

Vocabulary-in-context questions require using surrounding words and sentences to
infer meaning. Clues may include definitions, examples, contrasts, cause-effect
relationships, or repeated ideas.

## Author's Purpose

Authors usually write to inform, persuade, entertain, explain, describe, or
reflect. Purpose can be inferred from structure, tone, evidence, and intended
audience.

## Text Structure

Common structures include chronological order, compare and contrast, cause and
effect, problem and solution, description, and claim-evidence-reasoning.
Recognising structure helps readers predict what information matters.

## Evidence-Based Answers

Good comprehension answers use evidence. Students should avoid choosing an
answer only because it sounds familiar. The best answer must be both true and
supported by the passage.

## Eliminating Wrong Options

Wrong options often include extreme language, ideas not mentioned in the text,
partly true statements, reversed cause-effect relationships, or answers that are
too broad or too narrow.

## Active Reading Strategy

Before reading, preview the title and question. During reading, mark the main
idea, transitions, repeated words, and contrast words. After reading, summarise
the passage in one or two sentences before answering detailed questions.

## Summary Writing

A summary should include the main idea and the most important supporting points,
without personal opinion or unnecessary examples. It should be shorter than the
original text and written in the student's own words.
