# Math Foundations

## Number Sense

Number sense means understanding how numbers behave, not only memorising
procedures. A student with strong number sense can estimate, compare quantities,
spot unreasonable answers, and choose efficient methods. Estimation is useful
before calculation because it creates an expected range for the answer.

## Order Of Operations

The standard order of operations is brackets or parentheses first, then
exponents, then multiplication and division from left to right, then addition
and subtraction from left to right. A common mistake is doing all multiplication
before all division even when division appears first from the left.

Example: 18 / 3 x 2 = 6 x 2 = 12.

## Fractions

A fraction represents part of a whole or a ratio between two quantities. To add
or subtract fractions, use a common denominator. To multiply fractions, multiply
numerators and denominators. To divide by a fraction, multiply by its reciprocal.

Key idea: simplify only by dividing the numerator and denominator by the same
non-zero number.

## Decimals

Decimals are another way to write fractions with denominators based on powers of
10. Place value is essential: in 3.47, the 4 is tenths and the 7 is hundredths.
When multiplying decimals, multiply as whole numbers first and then place the
decimal point according to the total number of decimal places.

## Percentages

A percentage means parts per hundred. The core formula is:

percentage = part / whole x 100

To find a percentage of a number, convert the percentage to a decimal and
multiply. For example, 15% of 80 = 0.15 x 80 = 12.

## Percentage Change

Percentage change measures increase or decrease relative to the original value:

percentage change = (new value - original value) / original value x 100

Use a positive result for increase and a negative result for decrease. Students
often make the mistake of dividing by the new value instead of the original
value.

## Ratios

A ratio compares two or more quantities. The ratio 3:2 means for every 3 units
of the first quantity, there are 2 units of the second quantity. Ratios can be
scaled by multiplying or dividing every part by the same number.

Example: If boys:girls = 3:2 and there are 20 students total, then 5 parts = 20,
so 1 part = 4. Boys = 12 and girls = 8.

## Proportions

A proportion says two ratios are equal. If a / b = c / d, then ad = bc. This is
called cross multiplication. Proportions are useful for maps, recipes, similar
triangles, unit rates, and speed problems.

## Unit Rates

A unit rate tells how much of one quantity corresponds to one unit of another.
For example, if a student solves 24 questions in 3 hours, the unit rate is 8
questions per hour.

## Integers

Integers include positive numbers, negative numbers, and zero. Adding a negative
number is the same as subtracting. Subtracting a negative number is the same as
adding. Multiplying or dividing two numbers with the same sign gives a positive
answer; different signs give a negative answer.

## Factors And Multiples

A factor divides a number exactly. A multiple is the result of multiplying by a
whole number. Prime numbers have exactly two positive factors: 1 and themselves.
The greatest common factor helps simplify fractions. The least common multiple
helps add fractions with different denominators.

## Word Problem Strategy

For word problems, students should first identify what is being asked, list the
known values, define the unknown, choose an operation or equation, solve, and
check whether the answer is reasonable. Units should be carried through the
solution because units often reveal wrong operations.
