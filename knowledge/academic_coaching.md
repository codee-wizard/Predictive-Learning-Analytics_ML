# Academic Coaching

## Diagnostic Coaching

A good study coach first identifies the student's goal, current level, weak
areas, deadline, available time, and learning preferences. Advice should become
more specific as more student data becomes available.

## At-Risk Learners

At-risk learners need fundamentals, confidence, and consistent short practice.
The coach should reduce overload, focus on one concept at a time, include
worked examples, and use frequent checks for understanding. Recommended
activities include basic drills, mistake review, guided examples, and daily
revision.

## Average Learners

Average learners usually benefit from structured practice and gradual challenge.
The coach should mix concept review with moderate problems, introduce timed
practice, and encourage reflection on recurring mistakes. These students often
improve quickly when they move from passive reading to active problem solving.

## High-Performing Learners

High-performing learners need depth, challenge, and transfer tasks. The coach
should include multi-step problems, edge cases, explanation tasks, and challenge
projects. These students benefit from teaching others, solving unfamiliar
problems, and comparing multiple methods.

## Study Plan Design

A useful study plan should include clear daily goals, estimated time, practice
tasks, review blocks, and a feedback loop. Plans should avoid vague activities
like "study algebra" and instead say "solve 10 linear equation problems and
write down every error."

## Active Recall Routine

A strong active recall session starts with a closed-book attempt, then a check
against notes, then correction of mistakes, then a second attempt. The student
should record what they missed and revisit it later.

## Spaced Repetition Routine

A spaced repetition routine reviews material after increasing gaps. For a
student preparing for a test, a practical pattern is Day 1 learn, Day 2 recall,
Day 4 mixed practice, Day 7 timed quiz, and Day 14 cumulative review.

## Error Log

An error log should contain the problem, wrong answer, correct answer, type of
mistake, and prevention rule. Mistake types include concept gap, careless error,
forgotten formula, weak reading, time pressure, and incomplete working.

## Motivation

Motivation improves when goals are specific and progress is visible. Instead of
"get better at math", use "solve 15 quadratic equations with at least 80%
accuracy by Friday." The coach should praise effort, strategy, and improvement
rather than fixed ability.

## Exam Strategy

Good exam strategy includes reading instructions carefully, attempting easier
questions first, marking difficult questions for review, showing working, and
checking answers if time remains. Students should practise under timed
conditions before the actual exam.

## Feedback Style

Student feedback should be specific, kind, and actionable. It should identify
what went well, what needs improvement, and the next small step. The coach
should avoid overwhelming the student with too many corrections at once.
