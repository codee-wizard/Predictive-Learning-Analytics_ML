# Statistics And Probability

## Data

Data are collected observations or measurements. Quantitative data are numeric,
such as scores or heights. Qualitative data describe categories, such as lunch
type or transport method.

## Mean

The mean is the arithmetic average. Add all values and divide by the number of
values. Mean is sensitive to outliers. If one value is extremely high or low,
the mean may not represent a typical value well.

## Median

The median is the middle value when data are ordered. If there are two middle
values, the median is their average. Median is resistant to outliers and is
often better for skewed data.

## Mode

The mode is the most frequent value. A dataset can have no mode, one mode, or
multiple modes. Mode is useful for categorical data.

## Range

Range is maximum minus minimum. It gives a quick sense of spread but depends
only on two values, so it can be affected strongly by outliers.

## Interquartile Range

The interquartile range is Q3 - Q1. It measures the spread of the middle 50% of
the data. It is useful for identifying outliers and understanding variability.

## Standard Deviation

Standard deviation measures how far values typically are from the mean. A small
standard deviation means values are close to the mean. A large standard
deviation means values are more spread out.

## Graphs

Bar charts compare categories. Line graphs show change over time. Histograms
show distribution of numerical data. Scatter plots show relationships between
two variables. Box plots show median, quartiles, spread, and possible outliers.

## Correlation

Correlation measures association between two variables. Positive correlation
means both tend to increase together. Negative correlation means one tends to
increase as the other decreases. Correlation does not prove causation.

## Probability Basics

Probability measures likelihood and ranges from 0 to 1. A probability of 0
means impossible. A probability of 1 means certain.

probability = favourable outcomes / total equally likely outcomes

## Complement Rule

The complement of an event is the event not happening. The complement rule is:

P(not A) = 1 - P(A)

This is useful when it is easier to calculate what should not happen.

## Independent Events

Two events are independent if one event does not affect the probability of the
other. For independent events:

P(A and B) = P(A) x P(B)

## Dependent Events

Two events are dependent if one event changes the probability of the other. In
dependent probability, update the sample space after the first event.

Example: Drawing two cards without replacement is dependent because the first
draw changes the deck.

## Mutually Exclusive Events

Mutually exclusive events cannot happen at the same time. For mutually exclusive
events:

P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)

If events can overlap, subtract the overlap to avoid double counting.

## Expected Value

Expected value is the long-run average outcome. Multiply each outcome by its
probability and add the results. It does not always equal an actual possible
outcome.

## Study Tip For Statistics

Students should always ask: What does this number mean in context? Statistics
answers are incomplete if they do not connect back to the real situation.
