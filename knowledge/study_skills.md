# Study Skills

## Active Recall

Active recall means trying to retrieve an answer from memory before checking
notes. It is stronger than rereading because the act of retrieval shows the
student what they truly know. Good active recall tasks include blank-page
summaries, flashcards, closed-book problem solving, and teaching a concept out
loud.

## Spaced Repetition

Spaced repetition means reviewing material after increasing time gaps. A useful
beginner schedule is same day, next day, three days later, one week later, and
two weeks later. Difficult topics should appear more often until the student can
solve them accurately without hints.

## Mistake Review

A mistake log should record the question, the student's wrong approach, the
correct method, and the reason the mistake happened. Common reasons include
concept gaps, careless arithmetic, misreading the question, weak formula recall,
and poor time management. The next study session should start with two or three
problems from the mistake log.

## Exam Preparation

Effective exam preparation combines concept review, timed practice, and error
analysis. Students should first repair weak fundamentals, then practise mixed
questions, then attempt timed mock tests. After each mock test, the student
should classify mistakes and revise the highest-frequency error type first.

## Weekly Planning

A strong weekly study plan should have one main focus per session, a short
review of previous material, a practice block, and a reflection step. For
students who feel overwhelmed, shorter daily sessions are usually better than
rare long sessions.
